How the programme worked

Every Thursday for five weeks, LSETF beneficiaries received a new quiz via WhatsApp. There were no mandatory session times, no physical location to show up to. Participants could complete the quiz whenever they had a few minutes — on the bus, at the market, during a lunch break.

Registration happened entirely through WhatsApp. A short conversation: name, business type, location, main challenge. Most participants finished in under two minutes.

Quiz questions covered practical business topics — record keeping, pricing, business registration, understanding profit margins. Each question had three answer options as buttons. No typing required.

What we expected

Honestly, we expected completion rates in the 40 to 50% range. That is roughly where similar programmes land. People are busy. Traders especially have unpredictable days.

The thing that surprised us most was not the completion rate. It was that participants were messaging the bot between quizzes, asking questions that had nothing to do with the week's topic.

What actually happened

Final completion rate across the five sessions was 89%. We define completion as finishing at least four of the five quizzes with a passing score.

Two things seemed to drive this. First, the leaderboard. Participants checked their ranking multiple times between quizzes. Several mentioned specific people they were trying to stay ahead of. Second, the AI explanations on wrong answers used each participant's own business type as the example — not a textbook definition.

What did not work

Some participants found the button-based interface confusing at first. A few tried typing their answers instead of tapping the buttons, which the bot did not handle gracefully in early sessions. We fixed this, but it was a friction point.

A small number of participants had connectivity interruptions mid-quiz. We added a resume quiz feature after the second session.

What we would do differently

We would build in a short orientation before the first quiz. Some participants did not know what to expect and dropped off early simply because the format was unfamiliar.

The honest summaryAn 89% completion rate is encouraging, but we are cautious about reading too much into one programme, one LGA, five sessions. We are running more programmes to see whether the numbers hold. What we feel more confident about is the qualitative feedback: participants consistently said the format fit into their lives in a way that classroom sessions had not.

How EmpowerBot started

EmpowerBot was designed and built by ELP Foundation — Empowerment for Local People Foundation — independently. It grew out of frustration with existing MSME training approaches: classroom sessions that were hard to attend, handouts people did not keep, and almost no way to know whether anyone had actually learned anything.

The first version ran as a small pilot in 2024. A single WhatsApp number. A handful of quiz questions. A small group of traders in Alimosho who agreed to try it. The data from that pilot — especially the unexpectedly high completion rates — made us want to reach more people. That is where LSETF came in.

What LSETF brings as our first partner

The Lagos State Employment Trust Fund already had relationships with a large number of micro and small enterprises across Lagos State through its grants and loans programmes. LSETF became EmpowerBot's first programme partner — not as a co-builder, but as the organisation that brought its beneficiaries to the platform. They identify and refer participants from their network. ELP Foundation runs everything else.

Over 1,000 LSETF beneficiaries have now been trained through EmpowerBot across Alimosho, Agege, Ikorodu, Epe and other Lagos LGAs. The number is real. We track it at individual participant level.

How the partnership works in practice

LSETF identifies cohorts of beneficiaries eligible for capacity building support. ELP Foundation plans and delivers the programme — designing quiz modules, briefing facilitators, setting up the WhatsApp system, and managing data collection. After each programme, ELP Foundation produces an M&E report for LSETF showing completion rates, knowledge outcomes, and participant feedback.

Mrs Faith Oyeneye, ELP's lead facilitator, works directly with participants across LGAs. She knows many by name. That personal relationship — someone who shows up every Thursday and knows whether you have been keeping up — matters alongside the technology.

What remains ELP Foundation's

EmpowerBot — the quiz engine, the AI explanation system, the admin dashboard, the M&E data infrastructure — is built and maintained by ELP Foundation. LSETF is a programme partner, not a platform owner. The system can be licensed to other partners and deployed in other contexts. The LSETF work is the evidence that it works.

Why LGA context matters more than we expected

When we designed EmpowerBot, we assumed the programme would be largely consistent across locations. What we found was that LGA context shaped the experience in ways that mattered — different business types, different connectivity, different community structures.

Alimosho

Alimosho is one of Lagos's most densely populated LGAs with a very high concentration of market traders and informal businesses. Our first significant LSETF cohort here had a wide mix — food vendors, tailors, provisions sellers, petty traders, phone repair technicians. Completion rates were strong and the leaderboard dynamic was particularly intense.

Agege

Agege is where we ran our most documented programme. The LGA has a strong community character; many participants from the same market knew each other before the programme started. That social network probably contributed to the engagement levels we saw. The literacy range was also wider here, which pushed us to simplify question language.

Ikorodu

Ikorodu has a more dispersed geography — participants were less likely to know each other and the facilitator role was more important as a connective thread. Business types skewed more toward small-scale production and service businesses, which meant retail trading examples felt slightly less immediately relevant. We have since added more manufacturing-context examples to the AI prompting.

Epe

Epe presented a different connectivity context. Data access is less reliable in parts of Epe, and some participants experienced interruptions mid-quiz. We introduced the resume quiz feature partly in response. Fishing and agriculture-adjacent businesses common in Epe were also underrepresented in our original content — we are developing additional modules for these business types.

The platform is consistent across LGAs. The programme needs to flex. What works in Agege needs some adjustment to work as well in Epe.

What this means for future deployments

We now spend more time in the planning phase understanding the specific LGA context — what kinds of businesses dominate, what connectivity looks like, what community structures exist. The technology is the easy part. The contextual fit requires attention.

A clean sweep

Amina registered on the first day the programme opened. Her business — a provisions shop she has run for six years — meant she already had practical exposure to most of the topics the quizzes covered. But practical experience and quiz performance do not always track together. She got every question right.

What was notable was not just the score. It was that she finished each quiz within the first few hours of it being released — consistently the first in the cohort to complete each session.

What her facilitator observed

Mrs Faith Oyeneye had noticed Amina early. "She was the one encouraging others in the WhatsApp group," she said. "When someone said a question was too hard, Amina would say — just try it, the bot will explain."

Amina used the AI explanations even on questions she got right — going back to check the reasoning behind correct answers. This is unusual behaviour that we had not anticipated the platform being used for.

"I already knew some of these things from running my shop. But now I know why they are true — not just that they work."

What this tells us — and what it does not

Amina came in with more prior business knowledge than many participants and was clearly motivated in a way that not everyone is. A 100% score does not tell us what she will do differently in her business. It tells us she engaged seriously with the material — which is a meaningful start.

We hope to follow up with participants like Amina a few months after the programme to understand whether anything actually changed in how they operate. That is the harder and more important question.

How it started

The leaderboard was not in the original programme design. It came up during a team conversation about how to add a social dimension to what was otherwise a fairly solitary experience — a person alone with their phone, doing quizzes.

The idea was simple: after each quiz, participants could check their ranking within their cohort. Score, position, the names of the people above and below them.

What we noticed

Within the second session, participants were messaging the bot to check the leaderboard between quizzes. In post-programme conversations, the leaderboard came up unprompted more than any other feature. One participant who finished in the top three said she had stayed up late to complete the fourth quiz because she had seen that someone else was close to her score.

Traders are competitive by nature. They negotiate prices every day. When we gave them something to compete on, they did not need much encouragement to engage with it.

The honest caveat

We do not know with certainty that the leaderboard caused higher completion rates. It is plausible that the people who engaged with it were already more motivated. We have not run a version without a leaderboard to compare. What we do feel confident about is that it made the programme feel more social and more alive.

When this feature is not appropriate

A public leaderboard adds a social dimension that not everyone is comfortable with. In a more sensitive programme context — focused on financial difficulties or business challenges — publicly ranking participants could create discomfort. The feature is not universally appropriate. It depends on what you are running and who you are running it for.

Our first assumption was wrong

We assumed most drop-offs were about motivation — that participants who did not complete just were not interested enough. This framing put the problem on the participant side rather than the programme side, which in hindsight was a convenient assumption.

What the data showed

About a third of non-completers dropped off after the first session, before the second quiz was released. These were most likely linked to the registration experience — something that did not click, or expectations that did not match the reality of the programme.

The second-largest group dropped off between sessions three and four. Looking at their quiz timing, several had completed quizzes quite late in the session window — suggesting they were already stretched for time. A smaller group completed most sessions but missed the final quiz due to specific circumstances: travel, a family event, a busy week at the market.

One participant who did not finish messaged the facilitator weeks later to ask if they could still complete the last quiz. They had not dropped out by choice — they had just had a difficult month.

What this changed for us

We extended the session window, giving participants more days to complete each quiz. We also built a catch-up option for participants who miss a session — rather than being locked out, they can complete missed quizzes in a separate window before the programme ends.

We are still measuring whether these changes affect completion rates meaningfully. The honest answer is we do not know yet.

The pattern we noticed

We identified eight pairs or small groups of participants who knew each other before the programme — market neighbours, friends, women from the same cooperative. Across all eight groups, completion rates were higher than the cohort average. We are cautious about reading too much into this since people who sign up with friends may already be more motivated.

How the leaderboard changed things

What was clearer was the leaderboard dynamic within these pairs. Several were explicitly competing. One pair — neighbours who sold produce from adjacent stalls — each scored above 95% in the final two sessions. Their facilitator told us they had been texting each other about the quizzes between sessions.

"She beat me in week three. I made sure to finish week four first." — participant who scored second in the cohort overall

What this suggests for programme design

Encouraging group sign-ups — not mandating them, but making it easy for friends to join together — might improve engagement. We are experimenting with a refer-a-colleague prompt during registration for the next programme.

This is a small observation from one programme. We have eight pairs of data points, not a controlled trial. We are treating it as an interesting signal, not a proven finding.

What we noticed in the data

About a third of our Agege cohort observed Ramadan. Looking at quiz completion times, a clear pattern emerged: this group was significantly less likely to complete quizzes during typical daytime market hours, and significantly more likely to complete them in the evening — particularly between 9pm and midnight.

Completion rates for this group stayed high. The flexible, on-your-own-schedule format of the WhatsApp quiz turned out to be a genuine advantage here.

Where it created friction

The one area where timing caused a real problem was the live facilitation session on Thursday mornings. Participants who were fasting and managing a market stall from dawn were sometimes too tired by mid-morning to engage fully. Attendance at the physical component dipped for this group mid-programme.

We should have anticipated this and offered an alternative session time or a recording. We did not, and that was a gap in our planning.

The WhatsApp quizzes were self-paced and worked well. The in-person component did not account for the reality of participants' schedules during a religious observance. We need to fix that.

What we are changing for future programmes

We now flag potential religious observance periods during programme planning and adjust facilitation session timing where possible. We are also building a lightweight recording option for the live facilitation component so participants who cannot attend in person can still access it.

The original setup

When we first built the AI explanation feature, the output was in formal English. Correct. Clear. Technically accurate. And, based on the feedback we were getting, not particularly useful to many of the people reading it.

Participants would get a question wrong, receive an explanation, and move on. Very few asked follow-up questions. We assumed this meant they had understood. It turned out that for many, it meant the explanation had not landed at all.

What changed when we switched to Pidgin

We adjusted the AI prompt to respond in Nigerian Pidgin when the participant's profile indicated they were in Nigeria, and to use locally relevant examples — Naira amounts, specific market types, familiar business scenarios.

The difference in engagement was noticeable quickly. Participants started replying to the explanations — asking things like "but what if I no get the money?" or "so for my type of shop, which one better?"

"Make I explain am" hits differently than "Let me explain." It is the same instruction, but one sounds like a friend talking, and one sounds like a manual.

Why this probably matters

We are careful not to overclaim here. We did not run a controlled experiment comparing Pidgin and English groups with all other variables held constant. But the follow-up question rate — which we track as an indicator of engagement — roughly doubled after the switch. That is a meaningful enough signal to take seriously.

What the AI still gets wrong

The AI's Pidgin is not perfect. It occasionally uses phrases that feel slightly off, or defaults to more formal constructions when the topic gets complex. We are also finding that Naira examples sometimes feel slightly generic — the amounts or scenarios do not always match the specific business type. We are improving the context-passing to make examples more specific.

What the participant said

Fatimah, a tailor running a small shop in Ikorodu, got a question about pricing strategy wrong. The AI gave her an explanation about cost-plus pricing that included an example using fabric costs.

She messaged back: "The example no make sense for my type of work. I charge by the job, not by how much fabric I use." She was correct. The AI had given a reasonable general explanation, but the example did not fit custom tailoring, where labour and skill are the primary cost, not materials.

How we handled it

The bot, at that point, had no way to gracefully acknowledge being wrong. It offered a generic follow-up explanation that also missed the point. Fatimah stopped engaging with that question and moved on.

We only learned about this because her facilitator mentioned it in the weekly debrief. That was a problem — if a participant corrects the AI and gets a non-answer, they are likely to lose confidence in the explanations generally.

Fatimah's feedback was one of the most useful things we received from the entire programme. We just were not set up to capture it properly.

What changed

We built a simple feedback mechanism: after any AI explanation, participants can respond with a thumbs down emoji. The bot acknowledges the feedback, offers an alternative explanation, and flags the exchange for manual review. We also updated the pricing module to include service business examples alongside product-based examples.

The broader point

Participants often know things the AI does not. That is not a flaw to hide — it is information worth capturing. Treating participant pushback as useful feedback rather than a support problem is a design choice that takes deliberate effort to maintain.

What participants actually ask

The most common off-topic questions fall into a few categories: tax obligations, loan and credit questions, supplier disputes, and regulatory questions about licences. These are real business problems that matter to the participants asking them. They are also questions where a confident wrong answer could cause real harm.

What the bot does

The bot is designed to acknowledge the question, give general orientation where it can — explaining concepts rather than giving specific advice — and point participants toward appropriate resources. For legal and regulatory questions it refers to CAC or SMEDAN. For tax questions, FIRS. For financial decisions, it encourages participants to talk to someone who knows their specific situation.

One participant asked: "Why can't you just tell me the answer?" It is a fair question. The honest response is: because we do not know your specific situation, and getting it wrong matters.

Where we do engage

For questions closely related to quiz content — essentially, "can you explain more about what we just covered" — the bot engages fully. The line we are drawing is between education (explaining concepts and principles) and advice (recommending specific actions in specific situations). The former is something we can do reasonably well. The latter requires knowing things about a participant's situation that we do not know.

Why we are writing this

There is a tendency in product writing to describe AI capabilities in the most favourable light and mention limitations briefly if at all. We want to do the opposite here — partly because it is more honest, and partly because understanding the failure modes is genuinely useful for anyone thinking about deploying AI in a training context.

The most common problem: confident wrongness

Large language models can state incorrect information with the same confident tone they use for correct information. In a business training context, this is a real concern. If the AI confidently gives a wrong example about liability, a participant who trusts the explanation has been actively misled.

We address this in two ways. First, the quiz questions and correct answers are fixed — the AI is only generating explanations for wrong answers, which gives us more control over the factual core. Second, we have constrained the AI's scope: it is prompted to explain within a defined framework rather than freely generating business advice.

We review a sample of AI explanations each week. This catches patterns we would not otherwise see — topics where the model consistently struggles, or types of questions where the explanations go off track.

Context that does not quite fit

The AI uses participant profile information to personalise explanations. This works well when the categories are broad enough that the model has seen relevant training data. It works less well for very specific or unusual business types. A participant who runs a small logistics operation gets less useful personalisation than a provisions shop owner, because the former is less common in the model's training data.

What we are improving

We are building a feedback mechanism so participants can flag AI explanations that felt unhelpful or wrong. We are also building evaluations that test the AI against a set of known-correct explanations for common wrong answers. This gives us a more structured way to track quality over time.

The case against multiple-choice

Multiple-choice questions are a fairly blunt instrument for assessing understanding. A participant who selects the correct answer from three options has not necessarily demonstrated that they can apply the concept in their own work. With three options, the expected score for someone answering randomly is 33%. Scoring 50% could reflect minimal real learning. We are aware of this.

Why we use multiple-choice anyway

Three reasons: accessibility, scale, and consistency. Open-ended answers require reading, evaluating, and responding to each participant's individual response — at 250 participants across five sessions, that is not feasible without a team of evaluators or a much more sophisticated AI assessment system than we currently have.

WhatsApp's button-based interface also makes multiple-choice feel natural. The interaction requires no typing, which is a significant accessibility benefit for participants with limited literacy or who find typing on a small keyboard slow and effortful.

We are not claiming multiple-choice is the best way to assess understanding. We are saying it is the most practical way to assess it at scale, in a WhatsApp context, right now.

What we do to partially compensate

We ask participants to engage with follow-up conversations when they get questions wrong. The AI explanation is not just telling them the right answer — it is prompting them to think through why the right answer is right. We also look at patterns across questions rather than treating each in isolation. A participant who consistently gets similar types of questions wrong is telling us something different from one who makes scattered errors.

What we were reporting

Like many capacity building programmes, our early reporting focused heavily on process indicators. Number of training sessions held. Number of participants registered. Hours of training delivered. Attendance rates. These numbers were easy to capture and felt meaningful. But when we started having more candid conversations with funder contacts, a pattern emerged: they were politely acknowledging the numbers while clearly wanting something different.

What they were actually asking

The underlying question — the one that matters for continued investment — is simpler and harder at the same time: did the participants actually learn anything? And if they did, did it change how they operate their business?

One programme officer told us, very directly, that attendance figures were essentially table stakes — the minimum required to show the programme ran. What mattered was whether anything changed for participants as a result.

What EmpowerBot changed

Because every quiz response is captured automatically, we can generate outcome data that would previously have required significant manual effort. We can show score distributions at individual and cohort level. We can show which concepts participants struggled with and whether re-attempts improved scores.

This is not the same as proving business behaviour change — we want to be clear about that. A higher score on a quiz about record keeping does not tell us that a trader is now actually keeping better records. But it tells us something meaningful: that participants engaged with the material and demonstrated understanding in a structured way.

The problem with outcome KPIs for short programmes

A five-week training programme cannot credibly claim to improve business revenues, increase employment, or reduce business failure rates. These outcomes are shaped by dozens of factors — economic conditions, market access, capital availability — that a training programme does not control. Committing to them as KPIs is setting up to report misleading results.

The most honest KPI for a short training programme is probably: did participants learn what we intended them to learn? Everything else is a bigger claim that requires longer evidence.

What short programmes can reasonably measure

Knowledge change is measurable through pre- and post-assessments. Engagement is measurable — completion rates, quiz participation rates, follow-up question rates. Satisfaction is measurable through participant feedback. For a typical five-week programme, we suggest: completion rate target (ours is 75%+), average post-programme quiz score, score improvement from session one to five, and participant satisfaction rating collected at programme close.

How to have this conversation with funders

Most funders, in our experience, understand and appreciate honest framing. The conversation tends to go better when you lead with what the programme can reliably deliver, acknowledge what it cannot, and propose a follow-up mechanism for longer-term outcome tracking if feasible. What lands badly is promising outcomes and then not delivering them.

What training can and cannot do

Training changes knowledge. Whether it changes behaviour is a different question, with a more uncertain answer. The gap between knowing something and doing it is well-documented. People know they should exercise more. They know record keeping matters for their business. Knowing is not the hard part. Changing habits in a context where life constantly competes for attention is the hard part.

A five-week training programme, however good, operates at the knowledge end of this chain. We can reliably improve what participants understand about business topics. We cannot reliably change their daily practices — at least not from training alone.

What we deliver is documented knowledge change in a structured setting. Whether that translates to different business behaviour is a question that requires longer follow-up than most programmes conduct.

Why we are saying this out loud

Because the alternative — implying that training leads to behaviour change without being clear about the evidence — does the sector a disservice. Programmes get funded on the assumption that they produce outcomes they often cannot demonstrate. When the outcomes do not appear, it generates scepticism about training programmes generally, including well-designed ones.

What we are trying to do about it

We are building a lightweight follow-up module — three brief check-ins over the three months after programme completion, asking participants about specific practices the training covered. Not a full evaluation, but enough to get some signal on whether the knowledge is being applied. We will share what we find.

The obvious alternative

The obvious alternative to WhatsApp was a dedicated app. An app would give us more control over the interface, more flexibility, and a cleaner design. Several people we talked to early in the project suggested this route. We decided against it for one straightforward reason: an app requires a download step, and that download step is a significant barrier for the population we were trying to reach.

The download problem

For a trader in a Lagos market, downloading an unfamiliar app involves a series of decisions: Does this app cost money? Will it use too much storage? Is it safe? Do I need WiFi? These are real questions that create real friction, especially for people who are cautious about unfamiliar technology. WhatsApp removes almost all of that friction. The vast majority of our target participants already have it installed, already know how to use it, and already trust it.

We wanted participants' first experience to be the training, not the setup. WhatsApp makes that possible.

What we gave up

Building on WhatsApp means working within its constraints. We cannot do certain things — rich media, complex interactive elements, certain kinds of personalisation — as easily as we could in a native app. We also depend on the WhatsApp Business API, which has its own pricing structure, rate limits, and terms of service we have no control over. That is a real dependency risk.

Why we still think it was the right call

Adoption is the hardest problem in any training programme. If participants do not show up, nothing else matters. By starting with a platform participants already use, we reduced the adoption barrier to near zero. That tradeoff — less design control in exchange for lower friction — felt right for the context we were building for.

Version one was built for the wrong user

Our first admin dashboard was built by people who like data. It had participant-level drilldowns, cohort comparison charts, score distribution histograms, and export options in three formats. It was genuinely impressive if you were the kind of person who wanted to spend an hour exploring programme data. Most programme managers are not that person.

What the actual questions were

After watching several programme managers use the dashboard and asking them what they were actually trying to find out, three questions kept coming up: How many people have completed this week's quiz? Who has not finished yet so I can follow up? What did people struggle with so I know what to address next session? Everything else was noise.

"I do not need six chart types. I need to know who is behind and what they are stuck on. That is it."

What we rebuilt

We rebuilt the dashboard around those three questions. The default view now shows a simple completion tracker, a list of participants who have not finished the current session with a one-tap WhatsApp message button, and the top three questions by failure rate. Everything else is still there — just not on the front page.

What this taught us

The lesson was not that programme managers do not want data. It is that the data needs to be immediately actionable. A completion rate is useful. A list of names to follow up with is more useful. A pre-written WhatsApp message template ready to send is most useful of all.

The original registration problem

Our first registration flow asked nine questions in sequence: name, phone number (redundant — we already had it), business name, business type, location, years in operation, main challenge, how they heard about the programme, and consent to data processing. Most participants took between four and eight minutes. Drop-off was heaviest after question four.

What we changed

We cut the question count from nine to five: name, business type, location, main challenge, and consent. We changed the tone of the questions — "What is your primary business category?" became "What kind of business do you run?" We added a progress indicator. We added a brief welcome message explaining what the programme is before asking any questions. We made the final confirmation message more specific — instead of "You are registered!" we sent "You are registered, Amina. Your first quiz opens on Thursday."

Every unnecessary question is a potential drop-off point. Every unclear question is a friction point. We had both.

The result

Drop-off from registration start to completion fell from approximately 40% to under 10%. We do not attribute all of this to any single change — it was the combination. But the question reduction was probably the most significant single factor.

What we are still figuring out

We lost some data we would like to have by removing certain questions. We are exploring whether we can collect this later — perhaps after session two when participants have already committed to the programme — rather than upfront at registration.
